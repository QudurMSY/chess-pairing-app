C.04.4.3 Lim System (effective till 31 January 2026)

The Lim system is an older FIDE‑approved Swiss pairing method, first adopted in 1987 and amended several times. It was used in some tournaments up to 31 January 2026.

General pairing rules

Awarding the bye. When an odd number of players must be paired, the pairing‑allocated bye is awarded to the lowest‑ranked player in the lowest scoregroup. The bye gives the points awarded for a win and no colour.

Pairing a scoregroup.

Compatibility. Two players who have not played each other are compatible if pairing them does not require either player to have the same colour in three consecutive rounds or to have three more games with one colour than the other.

Pairing order. Players with the same score form a score‑group. The median score‑group contains players whose score equals half the number of rounds played. Pairing begins with the highest scoregroup and proceeds downward until just before the median scoregroup, then continues from the lowest scoregroup upward to the median, which is paired last.

Transfers (floaters). Before pairing a scoregroup, any player who lacks a suitable opponent is transferred to a neighbouring group. Transfers occur when a player has played all others in the group, has too many games of one colour, or there are an odd number of players. The transferred player is termed a floater.

Proposed pairings. After necessary transfers, players are sorted by their pairing numbers. The top half are tentatively paired with the bottom half (e.g., 1 v n/2+1, 2 v n/2+2, etc.). These pairings are then examined for compatibility and colour.

Exchanges. If a proposed pairing results in players who have already met or breaks colour rules, the lower‑numbered player is swapped with another to find a suitable opponent.

Pairing a blocked median scoregroup. If the median scoregroup cannot be paired, the group is extended step by step: either the next lower scoregroup or the next higher scoregroup has its next pairing “cracked,” and its players are treated as additional floaters.

Floater selection rules. Detailed rules specify how to choose which player floats to a lower or higher group, aiming to balance colour assignments and rating differences. Generally, the lowest‑numbered player floats downward, while the highest‑numbered floats upward, subject to colour‑balance and rating restrictions.

The Lim system is more procedural than modern systems; it requires extensive transfers and exchanges to balance colours and avoid repeat pairings. Because of its complexity, it is seldom used today.

C.04.4.3 Lim System (effective from 1 February 2026)

The Lim system remains a FIDE‑approved pairing method after 1 February 2026. Its rules are essentially unchanged but are presented with clearer numbering.

0 General pairing rules

Awarding the pairing‑allocated bye. When there is an odd number of players in a bracket, the bye is awarded to the lowest‑ranked player in the lowest scoregroup.

Pairing a scoregroup.

Compatibility. Two players who have not played each other are compatible provided that pairing them does not give either player the same colour in three consecutive rounds or three more games of one colour than the other.

Scoregroup order. Players with the same score form scoregroups. Pairing begins at the highest scoregroup and moves downward until just before the median scoregroup; it then proceeds upward from the lowest scoregroup to the median, which is paired last.

Transfers (floaters). Before pairing, players without suitable opponents (because they have played all others in the group or have colour issues) are transferred to a neighbouring group, becoming floaters.

Proposed pairings and exchanges. Players in the group are arranged by pairing number; the top half are tentatively paired with the bottom half. If a proposed pairing is illegal, players are exchanged according to the exchange rules.

Blocked median groups. If the median scoregroup cannot be paired, the next lower or higher group’s pairing is cracked to supply additional floaters.

Floater selection. Detailed floater selection rules determine which players move to adjacent groups, taking into account colour balance and rating differences. When pairing downward, the lowest‑numbered player is typically chosen; when pairing upward, the highest‑numbered is chosen. Additional conditions apply for maxi‑tournaments (large events).

Because of its complexity and the manual steps required to transfer players and perform exchanges, the Lim system is now rarely used outside of historical contexts.
