C.04.5 Double‑Swiss Pairing System

The Double‑Swiss system is an innovative pairing method approved by FIDE. It combines elements of Swiss and knockout formats by pairing players for matches consisting of two games (one with each colour), rather than single games. This section summarises the core rules.

1 Introductory remarks and definitions

Matches instead of single games. Each pairing produces a match of two games; players face each other once with White and once with Black. A match can result in a win (1½ points), draw (1 point) or loss (½ point) depending on the outcome of the two games.

Tournament pairing number (TPN). TPNs are assigned as in the general handling rules.

Order for pairing. Participants are ranked by (1) score (match points) and (2) TPN. This order is used to form scoregroups.

Scoregroups and brackets. A scoregroup contains players with the same match score. A pairing bracket is an even‑sized group comprising resident players from the scoregroup and, if needed, upfloaters from lower scoregroups.

Pairing‑allocated bye (PAB). If there is an odd number of players, one player receives a bye worth as many points as a drawn match (usually one point). The player receives no opponent and no colour.

Floaters. A floater is a player who plays against an opponent with a different score. Downfloaters come from higher scoregroups; upfloaters come from lower scoregroups.

Colour definitions. A player is said to have a colour in a match if at least one game in the match was actually played with that colour.

2 Pairing criteria

Absolute criteria ensure legality: two players cannot meet twice; a player who has already received a bye or forfeit win cannot receive another bye; and colours must respect absolute preferences.

Completion criterion requires that a legal pairing exists for all unpaired players within a bracket.

Quality criteria aim to minimise the number of upfloaters, minimise score differences among upfloaters, avoid floaters who floated in the previous round and their opponents, and minimise repeated colour assignments.

3 Pairing procedure

Legal pairing. A pairing is legal when absolute criteria are satisfied and a completion criterion is met.

Top‑scoregroup pairing. During pairing, the top scoregroup is combined with a set of upfloaters (if necessary) to form a bracket, which is then paired. This process is repeated for subsequent scoregroups until all players are paired.

Round‑pairing outlook. The pairing process involves: (a) assigning the bye (Article 3.4), (b) selecting upfloaters (3.5), (c) pairing the bracket (3.6), and (d) repeating until the round is complete.

PAB assignment. The bye is assigned to the player who leaves a legal pairing, has the lowest score, has played the highest number of matches and has the largest TPN.

The Double‑Swiss system thus pairs players for two‑game matches, preserving Swiss principles while introducing match‑level scoring and carefully regulating floaters and colour assignments.
