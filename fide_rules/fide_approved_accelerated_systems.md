C.04.7 FIDE‑approved accelerated systems (effective from 1 February 2026)

From 1 February 2026 the accelerated systems section is recast in numbered articles but maintains the same purpose: to modify early Swiss pairings to produce more competitive games while preserving final rankings.

0 Preface

Early rounds in Swiss tournaments tend to produce predictable results; few games deviate from the stronger player winning. In title tournaments this predictability can prevent players from achieving norms.

An accelerated pairing adjusts the first rounds while working with existing Swiss systems. This is typically done by adding virtual points to stronger players to reshape early score brackets.

1 Baku Acceleration Method

1.1 Premise. Applicable when the scoring system awards points for wins equal to two draws; losses earn zero points. The method modifies early pairings by giving some players virtual points.

1.2 Initial groups division. Before round 1, participants are split into two groups, GA and GB. GA consists of the first half of the participants (rounded up to the nearest even number), and GB contains the rest.

1.3 Late entries. Players who enter after the first round are placed according to the general handling rules; the identity of the last GA participant remains the same.

1.4 Virtual points. The first half of the rounds (rounded up) are “accelerated rounds.” Before pairing each accelerated round, GA players receive virtual points equal to a win; these points are halved for the remaining accelerated rounds. GB players never receive virtual points.

1.5 Pairing score. A participant’s pairing score equals their real score plus virtual points. Pairing score determines scoregroups and affects the sorting of boards for pairings in accordance with the general handling rules.

The 2026 edition clarifies that the Baku method (and any future accelerated methods) may be applied in individual or team events, provided the scoring system aligns with the method’s assumptions. As with the earlier version, other accelerated methods may be approved if they demonstrate at least equal efficacy or greater simplicity.
