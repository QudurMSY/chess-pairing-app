07. Tie‑Break Regulations (version effective until 31 August 2023)
Choice of tie‑break system

The tournament regulations must specify the tie‑break system before play begins. If all planned tie‑breaks fail, the tie shall be resolved by drawing lots.

A play‑off (additional games) is the preferred method for breaking ties, but it may not always be practical because of time constraints. A list of commonly used non‑play‑off tie‑breaks is given in alphabetical order; players are ranked according to each system.

Play‑offs

For events using play‑offs, sufficient time must be allocated and the pairing system and rate of play must be determined in advance. Play‑offs are recommended primarily for deciding first place, championships or qualifying spots.

Commonly used tie‑break systems

The regulations describe several tie‑break methods. Organisers may choose one or a sequence of these to rank tied players or teams. Key systems include:

Buchholz (median, cut 1, cut 2): the sum of opponents’ scores; variations reduce the lowest one or two opponent scores.

Direct encounter: if all tied players have played one another, their head‑to‑head results determine the ranking.

Koya system: points scored against opponents who achieved 50% or more; it can be extended to include opponents with lower scores or reduced to exclude those with 50%.

Number of games won and number of games won with Black pieces: more wins, especially with Black, rank higher.

Number of games played with Black pieces: players who played more games with Black are favoured.

Sonneborn‑Berger: sum of the scores of opponents a player defeated plus half of the scores of opponents with whom he drew. Variants exist for double round‑robin tournaments and for team competitions.

Cumulative (progressive) score: the sum of a player’s cumulative scores after each round, and variations where the first one or more rounds are ignored.

Tie‑breaks in team competitions

Match points vs game points: In team events decided by game points, match points (e.g., 2 points for a match win, 1 point for a draw) serve as a tie‑break. In events decided by match points, total game points become the tie‑break.

Combined systems

The regulations list tie‑break systems that use both the player’s and opponents’ results (e.g., Sonneborn‑Berger, Koya, number of wins, direct encounter).

There are tie‑break systems using only a team’s own results (match points, game points, direct encounter).

Tie‑breaks based solely on opponents’ results include Buchholz and its variants, as well as various Sonneborn‑Berger formulas tailored to team tournaments.

Tie‑breaks using rating or performance

When all players are rated, additional tie‑breaks based on ratings may be used. A player who takes more than two unplayed games is considered to have a lower average rating of opponents than any player who completes more games.

These regulations provide tournament organisers with a comprehensive menu of tie‑break methods. The chosen method(s) must be announced in advance and applied consistently. If all selected tie‑breaks fail to separate players, the tie is resolved by lot
