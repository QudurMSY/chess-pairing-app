General Regulations for Competitions – Annex 1: Berger Table

The Berger table provides standard pairings for round‑robin tournaments. When there is an odd number of players, the highest number is considered a bye. For a double‑round event, the order of the last two rounds of the first cycle should be reversed to avoid three consecutive games with the same colour.

Pairings by number of players

The table lists which players face each other in each round for different tournament sizes. Below is a summary of the pattern:

3 or 4 players: round 1: 1–4, 2–3; round 2: 4–3, 1–2; round 3: 2–4, 3–1.

5 or 6 players: round 1: 1–6, 2–5, 3–4; round 2: 6–4, 5–3, 1–2; round 3: 2–6, 3–1, 4–5; round 4: 6–5, 1–4, 2–3; round 5: 3–6, 4–2, 5–1.

7 or 8 players: round 1: 1–8, 2–7, 3–6, 4–5; round 2: 8–5, 6–4, 7–3, 1–2; round 3: 2–8, 3–1, 4–7, 5–6; round 4: 8–6, 7–5, 1–4, 2–3; round 5: 3–8, 4–2, 5–1, 6–7; round 6: 8–7, 1–6, 2–5, 3–4; round 7: 4–8, 5–3, 6–2, 7–1.

9 or 10 players: the table specifies rounds 1–9 with predetermined pairings, e.g., round 1: 1–10, 2–9, 3–8, 4–7, 5–6; round 2: 10–6, 7–5, 8–4, 9–3, 1–2. Similar patterns continue through round 9.

11 or 12 players: predetermined pairings for rounds 1–11, starting with round 1: 1–12, 2–11, 3–10, 4–9, 5–8, 6–7. Subsequent rounds rotate opponents to ensure everyone plays everyone once.

13 or 14 players, 15 or 16 players: the table continues to list pairings for up to 14 or 16 players. For example, with 15–16 players, round 1: 1–16, 2–15, 3–14, 4–13, 5–12, 6–11, 7–10, 8–9. Each round rotates pairings so that all players meet over the course of the tournament.

In summary, the Berger table ensures a fair rotation of opponents in round‑robin tournaments. Organisers consult the full table to assign pairings for each round based on the number of participants. The highest number acts as a bye in odd‑player events, and double‑round tournaments should reverse the final two rounds of the first cycle
