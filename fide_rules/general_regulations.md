General Regulations for Competitions (FIDE Handbook C.05)
Preface and scope

The FIDE General Regulations for Competitions set out rules that apply alongside the FIDE Laws of Chess for all official FIDE events and FIDE‑rated competitions. National laws of the host country take precedence.

When other regulations do not cover a situation, these rules are definitive. They apply to three levels of events – official FIDE events (L1), events where FIDE titles/norms may be earned (L2) and other FIDE‑rated competitions (L3).

Chief Organiser (CO) and Chief Arbiter (CA)

The organising federation may appoint a Chief Organiser responsible for financial, technical and organisational matters. The CO must prepare event regulations, provide anti‑cheating equipment, enforce dress‑code rules, register the tournament with FIDE and supervise technical staff.

The Chief Arbiter is responsible for overseeing the competition in accordance with the Laws of Chess and these regulations. Before the event he checks the playing conditions and equipment and draws up any additional rules with the CO. During the event the CA keeps records, oversees the competition, maintains order and ensures player comfort. In case of disagreement between CA and CO on laws interpretation, the CA’s view prevails.

Playing hall and equipment

The playing venue should have a separate smoking area accessible from the playing area if possible. If smoking is prohibited, this must be announced in the regulations. All games must be played at the specified times.

Large digital clocks are required for FIDE events with 30 or more players; for smaller events a timed announcement is made before play.

Pairings

Round‑robin events must use the Berger tables (Annex 1) for pairings; adjustments may be made for double‑round events.

When pairings are restricted (e.g. players from the same federation should not meet in the last rounds), this must be announced in advance. The Varma tables (Annex 2) provide a method for restricted pairings for tournaments of 9–24 players.

Swiss‑system events must use a pre‑announced pairing system from the FIDE Swiss Rules (C.04).

Drawing of lots and withdrawals

The CA is responsible for drawing lots and making pairings. The draw for a round‑robin’s first round should be open to players and, in L1 events, held at least 12 hours before the first round. Players absent at the draw may still be included at the CA’s discretion.

If a player withdraws or is excluded before round 1 (or additional entries are added), published pairings remain unchanged; adjustments may be made only if they minimise changes. A player who is absent without notifying the arbiter is considered withdrawn unless a valid reason is given before the next pairing is published.

Round‑robin events: if a player withdraws and has completed less than half his games, his results remain for rating and historical purposes but are not counted in final standings; unplayed games are recorded as losses for the player and wins for opponents. If at least half of the games have been completed, all results remain and count.

Swiss events: when a player withdraws, only played games count for rating and tie‑breaks. Players should notify the CA before pairings if they cannot play a round. In L2/L3 events, two players without opponents after the round begins may be paired if both agree; a one‑time half‑point bye may be allowed with adequate notice.

Team competitions and the role of the captain

A team competition is one where the results of individual games contribute equally to a team’s final score.

The captain must submit the list of players for each round at a specified time; if the list is late, the top players from the squad list are used. In L1 events, more than half of the team must be present at the start of any round.

Captains may advise their players to accept or offer draws (unless regulations prohibit this) but must not otherwise interfere and must communicate through the arbiter. Captains can delegate their duties by informing the CA.

Conduct of players and discipline

Once a player accepts an invitation, they must play unless exceptional circumstances (force majeure) prevent participation.

Players should be suitably dressed and may be penalised for violating the dress code. Leaving without resigning or notifying the arbiter is considered poor sportsmanship and may incur penalties. Arranged results and eating at the board are prohibited.

Appeals procedure

Competitions must have an Appeals Committee appointed before the first round. It is recommended to have a chair, two members and two reserves from different federations. Members should be at least 21 years old and cannot participate in appeals involving themselves.

A player or official may appeal a ruling made by the CA or CO; appeals must be written, accompanied by a fee and submitted by a deadline. The fee is returned if the appeal is successful or considered reasonable.

Media regulations

Television cameras and photographers may be allowed provided they do not disturb players. Flash photography is generally restricted to the first ten minutes of round 1 and the first five minutes of subsequent rounds. Specific event regulations may modify these rules.

Invitations, registration and event information (L1 events)

Invitations to official competitions should be issued as early as feasible; the CO sends invitations through national federations and must first obtain approval from the FIDE President (for world championships) or Continental President (for continental championships).

Invitations must include comprehensive details: dates and venue, hotel arrangements, schedule (arrival, opening ceremony, technical meeting, etc.), rate of play, pairing and tie‑break systems, default time, draw‑agreement rules, expenses, prize fund, taxes, visa information, transport, number of participants, names of invitees, name of the CA, event website and contact details. Players can inform organisers of medical, dietary or religious requirements and disabilities.

Once issued, an invitation cannot be withdrawn if the player replies by the deadline; if the event is cancelled or postponed organisers must provide compensation. The CO must guarantee medical care and insurance for participants during the event.

Appointments of arbiters (L1 events)

For world events the Chief Arbiter is nominated by the FIDE President; for continental championships by the Continental President. The CA must hold an International Arbiter title (A or B) and have adequate experience. Other arbiters and staff are nominated by FIDE and/or the Organising Committee in consultation with the CA.
