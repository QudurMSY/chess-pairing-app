07. Tie‑Break Regulations (effective from 1 August 2024)

The 2024 revision replaces the previous tie‑break regulations and introduces structured play‑off and off‑the‑board tie‑break procedures. Approved by the FIDE Council on 29 July 2024, these rules apply to all FIDE‑rated competitions from 1 August 2024.

Scope and ranking of tied participants

The regulations apply to all FIDE‑rated events.

Tournament regulations must specify whether tied participants will share places or be ranked via a tie‑break method. If nothing is specified, the default is to use off‑the‑board tie‑breaks (Article 2.2.2) beginning with the first method listed by the Chief Organiser.

Methods of ranking ties

Play‑offs (Article 3) – over‑the‑board games to break ties. When a play‑off is used, the tournament regulations must state:

Which tied positions will be decided by play‑off (e.g., only first place).

Whether qualification for play‑offs depends on applying some or all tie‑breaks first.

The play‑off format (round‑robin, knockout, etc.), how pairing numbers and colours are allocated, time limits and schedules.

Off‑the‑board tie‑breaks (Article 4) – an ordered list of statistical criteria chosen by the Chief Organiser from Article 5. The Chief Arbiter must complete and publish the list before the tournament begins. Participants are ranked by the first tie‑break criterion; if ties remain, subsequent criteria are applied.

Categories of tie‑breaks

Article 4.3 classifies tie‑break evaluations into four types:

Type A (head‑to‑head data): based on a subset of games among the tied participants (e.g., direct encounter). The same type may appear multiple times in the list.

Type B (own results): based solely on the participant’s own results (e.g., number of wins, games with Black).

Type C (opponents’ final results): uses opponents’ final scores and can only be calculated after the round or tournament ends (e.g., Buchholz).

Type D (opponents’ prior known data): uses opponents’ ratings or earlier results (e.g., average rating of opponents).

When two participants play more than once, each encounter is treated separately for tie‑break calculations.

Tie‑break list (Article 5)

Article 5 provides an alphabetical list of tie‑breaks, each associated with a type and acronym. Tournament organisers select criteria from this list. Examples include:

Average of Opponents’ Buchholz (AOB) – Type C.

Average Rating of Opponents (ARO) – Type D.

Buchholz (BH) – Type C.

Direct Encounter (DE) – Type A.

Number of Games Played with Black (BPG) – Type B.

Number of Games Won (WIN) or Number of Games Won with Black (BWG) – Type B.

Sonneborn‑Berger (SB) – Type B/C.

Sum of Progressive Scores (PS) – Type B.

Tournament Performance Rating (TPR) and other rating‑based measures – Type D.

For team knock‑outs, specialised criteria such as Board Count (BC) are listed.

When designing a tie‑break order, organisers may mix and match criteria of different types. If, after exhausting the list, ties persist, they are resolved by drawing lots.

These updated regulations provide a flexible yet systematic framework for resolving ties. Organisers must publish their chosen tie‑break sequence in advance, and arbiters ensure it is applied consistently throughout the competition
